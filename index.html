Feast of the Black Nazarene
Location: Manila
Date: 9th of January

The Feast of the Black Nazarene commemorates the transfer of a dark wooden statue of Jesus Christ to its home in Quiapo Church. The image was originally brought from Mexico in 1606 by Augustinian Recollect friars and was initially housed in Bagumbayan (now Luneta), then moved to an Intramuros church before its final relocation to Quiapo. The feast is celebrated with a massive procession called the Traslacion, which draws millions of devotees annually.

  • 1606: The image of the Black Nazarene was brought to the Philippines from Mexico by Augustinian Recollect friars.
  • 1608: The image was moved from its initial location at St. John the Baptist Church in Bagumbayan to the Recollects' church in Intramuros.
  • 1621: The Cofradia de Jesus Nazareno, or the Confraternity of Jesus Nazarene, was founded.
  • 1767: On January 9, Manila Archbishop Basilio Sancho de Santas Justa y Rufina ordered the transfer of the image to Quiapo Church. This event is commemorated annually as the Translacion.
  • 1787: The image was canonically installed at Quiapo Church, which became known as the Minor Basilica of the Black Nazarene.
  • Later events: The image has survived numerous fires and earthquakes, further cementing its miraculous status for devotees. The devotion continues to grow, with the annual feast becoming one of the largest displays of religious fervor in the world.

Liberation of Manila
Location: Manila
Date: Every 3rd day of February

The "Liberation of Manila" is not a single, official, large-scale national festival, but rather a series of commemorative events and historical exhibits held annually in February to mark the anniversary of the Battle of Manila in 1945, one ofthe most brutal and tragic urban battles of World War II.

The events commemorate the month-long battle (February 3 - March 3, 1945) in which combined American and Filipino forces liberated the capital city from the Imperial Japanese Army.

The annual commemoration holds profound cultural significance as a solemn reminder of the immense human cost of war, the resilience of the Filipino people, and the enduring value of freedom and democracy.

Feast of the Sto. Niño
Location: Metro Manila
Date: Every third Sunday of January

The Feast of Sto. Niño in Metro Manila (NCR) celebrates the Holy Child Jesus, rooted in the 1521 arrival of Christianity when Magellan gifted the image to Cebu's Queen Juana, sparking devotion and dances like the Sinulog, with NCR celebrations like the Buling-Buling in Pandacan honoring this, featuring traditional dances and prayers, acknowledging the Sto. Niño as a protector and source of miracles, making it the oldest and oen of the most beloved devotions in the Philippines.

  • 1521: The devotion started when Ferdinand Magellan gave an image of the Child Jesus to hara Amihan (Queen Juana) of Cebu upon her baptism, marking the beginning of Christianity in the Philippines.
  • 1565: After Magellan's death, the image was miraculously found by Spanish conquistador Miguel Lopez de Legazpi amidst burnt ruins, reinforcing its miraculous status and leading to the construction of the Basicilica Minore del Santo Niño in Cebu.

The Sto. Niño became a powerful symbol of God's presence, humility, and protection, with devotees believing in its intercession for blessings and healing.
